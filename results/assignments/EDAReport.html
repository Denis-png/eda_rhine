0. Introduction

We named our Rhine river exploration as a trip to a new land. The destination of our trip was a proof of the following hypothesis, made by Middelkoop:

Higher winter discharge as a result of intensified snow-melt and increased winter precipitation, and lower summer discharge due to the reduced winter snow storage and an increase of evapotranspiration.

On the whole way to prove this assumption, we were using rivers runoffs, catchment areas, altitudes and variables for some particular cases. As a main tool we used the R language. Data was recorded from stations on the Rhine river. We had it from 17 station, which names were shortened for convenience. The period of records is since 1814 till 2016, but depending on our reasonings, periods were taken differently.

In all operations with a data were used libraries: data.table for formatting data as a tables and work with it and ggplot2 for plotting data. 3. Actual exploration

A way to explore something is to notice that it is relative to something else. And that is a way we did. We have been comparing our runoff at each station relatively to a catchment area of a station and an altitude. For example, stations based in mountains turned out to have smaller runoff than the others. But the opposite, stations which are the closest to the sea, had bigger runoff value. Important to remember about the time scale. Whole our data is sensitive to the time. We were comparing daily, monthly and annual runoff on each station, because sometimes the changes can be noticeable only in a large time scale, or it might turn out that there is no change at all and increase/decrease of runoff is natural. And finally season runoff. That was our destination point. We divided our data sets with runoff values depending on season (winter or summer) .
